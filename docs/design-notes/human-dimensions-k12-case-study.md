# Case study: when and how should the human dimensions of evolution enter a K-12 progression?

**Status:** Informative — a research artifact synthesizing real scholarship and real repository content, structured after the [Selection cross-domain case study](selection-cross-domain-case-study.md): state a claim, check it against independent evidence, report honestly what holds up and what doesn't. This document does **not** propose a resolution and does **not** modify any schema, vocabulary, or strand file. Per OECB design principle 7 (§3, §8.6 of the [specification](../oecb_specifications.md)), the disagreement it describes is one OECB is built to represent, not adjudicate.

**See it live:** the grade-band figures in "A quantitative look" below are hand-computed from the two LPM repos as they exist today; once Phase 4's analytics tooling and a Case Studies view exist in the Explorer app (tracked separately), this document will be updated to link the same numbers as a live, checkable view — the same pattern the Selection case study uses with `?lens=Selection`.

## The question, precisely

Within a K-12 scope and sequence for evolutionary biology — or for an interdisciplinary evolutionary/cultural-systems progression — when and how should the human dimensions of evolution (human behavior, human cognition, human culture, and, as a live extension, AI as a case of machine-culture coevolution) be introduced, and in what form at each grade band? This is not one question but at least three, worth keeping distinct:

1. **Timing** — at what grade band, if any, should human-specific examples first appear?
2. **Form** — when they do appear, are they named directly ("a human," "a person") or handled through structurally-parallel-but-unnamed framing (an unnamed "individual," a cross-species comparison that includes but doesn't single out humans)?
3. **Density** — introduced once as a capstone, or woven in repeatedly and reinforced across bands?

Two research literatures bear on this, and — this is worth stating up front, since it shapes how the rest of this document should be read — they are largely disconnected from each other right now. The evolution-education literature on human examples (Pobiner et al., Grunspan et al.) doesn't engage the K-12 AI-literacy literature (Casal-Otero et al., AI4K12) or the emerging scholarship on algorithms as cultural agents (Sánchez-Vera 2025), and vice versa. Nobody has yet written the paper that treats "when to introduce human evolutionary content" and "when to introduce AI-as-cultural-coevolution" as one integrated design question. That gap is real, not a failure of literature search — it's part of why this case study exists, and part of why `oe-interdisciplinary-k12`'s attempt to hold both under one strand is genuinely frontier territory rather than a synthesis of settled findings.

## Steelmanning both positions

### The case for holding human examples until general evolutionary literacy is established

Kampourakis (2020) documents a specific, durable failure mode: framing evolutionary outcomes in agentic or intentional terms — language like "in order to," or examples inviting a "why did this organism do X" reading — readily activates an intuitive "design stance," the folk-psychological habit of explaining biological form by appeal to purpose or intention. Once installed, that stance is difficult to dislodge and actively interferes with a mechanistic, population-level understanding of selection. Human examples are exactly the examples most likely to invite this framing, precisely because human behavior *is* often genuinely intentional in a way that (say) bacterial chemotaxis is not — so a human example, introduced before a student has a robust non-agentic causal model of selection to fall back on, is a plausible-sounding shortcut to the misconception Kampourakis is warning about. On this reading, the caution isn't "humans are a uniquely bad example" so much as "agency-adjacent examples are risky before the underlying mechanism is secure, and humans are the paradigm case of an agency-adjacent example."

Grunspan et al. (2021) supply an empirical echo of the same caution from a different angle: in a controlled comparison, a human-framed evolution lesson produced measurably *more* discomfort than an otherwise-identical non-human version, alongside higher engagement — a real cost, not merely a hypothetical one, and one that a student without an already-secure grasp of the underlying mechanism has fewer resources to work through productively.

### The case for early, repeated, developmentally-scaffolded engagement

Lehrer & Schauble (2012) show the opposite is also empirically supportable: elementary-age children can engage productively with the actual foundations of evolutionary thinking — variation, population thinking, selection as a population-level process — when given age-appropriate modeling tools, not necessarily human examples specifically, but genuine conceptual engagement well before secondary school. This cuts against any assumption that "early" automatically means "too abstract" or "developmentally premature": the limiting factor in their study was scaffolding design, not age.

Hanisch et al. (2025) make the stronger, more specific claim that organism agency itself — including human agency — can be *scientifically* integrated into evolutionary explanations, not merely tolerated as a pedagogical bridge to be dismantled later, through what they call "integrated causal reasoning." On this view, deferring agency-inclusive framing isn't a safety measure, it's a missed opportunity: it withholds a conceptually legitimate resource (behavior as a real causal factor shaping what gets selected, e.g. via niche construction) rather than protecting students from a misconception. Hanisch & Eirdosh (2023) extend this to the primary grades specifically, arguing evolutionary concepts are teachable — and pedagogically valuable — across the primary curriculum generally, not held back for secondary biology. 

### Why this isn't actually a symmetric disagreement about "when"

Read closely, the two camps aren't disagreeing about the same variable. Kampourakis's caution is about *causal framing* (does the example implicitly smuggle in agency/design language before the mechanism is secure), while Hanisch et al.'s case is about *whether agency, framed non-teleologically, is itself illegitimate content to exclude*. A curriculum could plausibly satisfy both: introduce agency-inclusive human examples early, but only ones engineered — the way `OE-STRAND-000223`'s "without requiring conscious intention" framing attempts — to avoid the design-stance trap Kampourakis identifies. Whether `OE-INTERDISCIPLINARY`'s actual strand content achieves that in practice, rather than merely intending to, is exactly what the next section checks.

### What depends on classroom context, with real numbers behind it

This is not a minor caveat. Berkman, Pacheco & Plutzer (2008) found high-school evolution instructional time ranging from roughly 3 to 15+ hours depending on the teacher, with a national mean of 13.7 hours — nearly a five-fold spread. Branch, Reid & Plutzer (2021) found comparably wide, often minimal, variation at the middle-school level specifically — the exact grade band where `OE-STRAND-000222`/`000223` sit. A "rich-scaffolding" argument and a "minimal-prior-exposure" argument can both be straightforwardly correct — for different classrooms that actually exist in the same country today. This is the empirical anchor for the trajectory mechanism introduced below: it isn't a hedge invented to avoid taking a position, it's a response to a documented, large, real distribution of classroom conditions.

## Grounding the comparison in the actual repos

### `bio-core-k12`: absence, not deferral

A direct search of `bio-core-k12/strands/*.yaml` for the word "human" returns zero matches, in any of the three strands or twelve substrands, across all four grade bands. Every example is a generic organism, a population, a species, a phylogeny, or — literally — a dinosaur (`OE-STRAND-000131`'s K-2 performance indicator). This is worth naming precisely, because it is easy to misread as an instance of "defer until general literacy is established" (the Kampourakis-aligned position above), and it is not that. `bio-core-k12`'s own README states the single-vocabulary, no-agency-concept design is deliberate and load-bearing — it exists specifically to keep the LPM structurally consistent with a decentralized-causation model of evolutionary explanation, as the intentional counterpoint to `OE-INTERDISCIPLINARY`'s agency-inclusive sibling. But that documented rationale is about *agency*, not about *humans as a taxon*. Nothing in `bio-core-k12`'s design record explains why humans specifically — as opposed to any other single species used as a recurring worked example — never appear, even in strictly non-agentic, decentralized-causal framing (e.g., human phylogeny, human common ancestry, human population genetics — all expressible without a single agency term). That is an absence with no recorded rationale, independent of which camp in the debate above is right. Phase 7 of this initiative proposes closing that gap, honestly labeled, not silently.

### `oe-interdisciplinary-k12`: real content, concentrated late, and structurally uneven

A grep of the same kind against `oe-interdisciplinary-k12/strands/*.yaml` finds exactly three literal uses of "human," and all three sit in 9-12 substrands:

- `OE-STRAND-000224` (Strand 2, 9-12, **`required: false`** — an elective/regional extension): "Places several organisms (bacterium, bird, chimpanzee, human) along an agency spectrum and justifies each placement," and "Compares biological niche construction (earthworms, beavers) with human-technological niche construction (cities, digital information environments)."
- `OE-STRAND-000234` (Strand 3, 9-12, **`required: true`**): "Explains how human technological niche construction (Strand 2) and cultural selection (Strand 1) jointly shape a contemporary information environment (e.g., a social media platform)."

Below 9-12, the concept Agency (`OE-CONCEPT-000211`) is introduced at K-2 (`OE-STRAND-000221`) and carried as `emphasis: primary` through every subsequent grade band's substrand (`000222`, `000223`, `000224`) — genuinely early and genuinely repeated, exactly the shape Hanisch et al.'s and Lehrer & Schauble's positions above would predict as favorable. But the concept is applied to unnamed "living things," "animals," and "a person" generically; it is never anchored to a human example by name until the elective 9-12 substrand. So the honest description is not "oe-interdisciplinary defers humans" or "oe-interdisciplinary introduces humans early" — it's both, depending on which of the three questions in the opening section ("timing," "form," "density") you're asking. The underlying *concept* is early and dense; the *named human instance* of that concept is late and, in the elective substrand's case, optional. That's a real, checkable finding, not a hedge.

It's also worth flagging that the AI/machine-culture-coevolution material is split unevenly between the two 9-12 substrands: `OE-STRAND-000224`, which has the more explicit "human" language, is the elective one; `OE-STRAND-000234`, which is required, carries the more developed coevolution claim (human niche construction + cultural selection jointly shaping "a contemporary information environment") but never uses the word "human" in a performance indicator naming a human individual — it stays at the level of technological/cultural systems. A student who skips the elective substrand encounters the systems-level AI-coevolution claim in `000234` without ever having worked through `000224`'s more concrete agency-spectrum comparison that would motivate it. Whether that's a sequencing gap worth fixing (recommend `000224`, don't leave it elective) or an intentional two-speed design (core systems claim for everyone, deeper individual-agency comparison for those who opt in) isn't something this document resolves — it's exactly the kind of open question the invitation at the end is for.

### The definitional drift, and why it matters here specifically

The Selection case study already flagged, as a small internal-consistency finding independent of any external standard, that `OE-CONCEPT-000211`'s formal vocabulary definition of Agency — "the capacity of an individual or system to **act intentionally** and make choices that influence outcomes, rather than responding passively to external forces" — reads more mentalistic than `OE-STRAND-000223`'s own pedagogical restatement three grade bands later: "a capacity to regulate behavior toward a goal-state, **without requiring conscious intention**." That drift matters more here than it did in the Selection case study, because any human-focused elaboration of Agency — exactly what Phase 7 of this initiative and any future RFC-driven strand work would produce — inherits whichever framing it's built on. "Act intentionally" is a much easier fit for human examples (where intention is uncontroversial) than for the earthworm/beaver niche-construction examples the same strand also uses; "regulate behavior toward a goal-state without requiring conscious intention" is the more defensible frame for a cross-species agency spectrum, but is also the framing more exposed to Kampourakis's design-stance critique if it's not applied carefully. Resolving which framing `OE-CONCEPT-000211` actually means — a vocabulary-level decision, not a strand-level one — should happen before, not after, any new human-anchored content is built on top of it.

## Context-specific trajectories: representing the disagreement as data

The findings above don't point to a single "correct" grade-band placement for human examples — they point to at least two internally coherent, evidence-backed designs that assume different things about the classroom a student is actually in. OECB's pluralism model already has a mechanism for exactly this situation at the vocabulary level (`BIO-CORE` vs. `OE-INTERDISCIPLINARY` as separate, non-adjudicated vocabularies). What doesn't yet exist is an equivalent mechanism *within* a single strand's sequencing — a way to say "this substrand has more than one defensible path through it, and which path applies depends on an explicit, named assumption about instructional context," without forking the whole strand into parallel LPMs.

This document proposes naming and formally defining two such trajectories, as a concept, for schema work to pick up in Phase 3:

- **Rich-scaffolding trajectory** — assumes ample instructional time (Berkman/Pacheco/Plutzer's higher end, 15+ hours), early exposure to evolutionary thinking well before the strand in question, and support for handling the discomfort Grunspan et al. document. Under this assumption, early, repeated, human-named engagement (closer to Hanisch et al.'s position) is defensible and arguably preferable — the developmental readiness Lehrer & Schauble demonstrate is available to be used.
- **Minimal-prior-exposure trajectory** — assumes evolution is thinly taught, or not reliably taught at all, before the grade band in question (Branch/Reid/Plutzer's documented reality for a large share of US middle schools). Under this assumption, Kampourakis's caution about introducing agency-adjacent human examples before the underlying non-agentic mechanism is secure carries more weight, favoring deferral or a more heavily scaffolded, later introduction.

These are not two competing LPMs and not a value judgment about which classroom is "better" — they're two coexisting, explicitly tagged paths through the same strand, each defensible under its own stated assumption, exactly mirroring how `BIO-CORE` and `OE-INTERDISCIPLINARY` coexist today without either being canonical. The concrete schema mechanism for tagging a substrand with one or more trajectories — a new field, or a documented convention on the existing `required`/`recommendedSequence` machinery — is Phase 3 scope and goes through full RFC review before anything is built on it; this document only establishes that the concept is needed and why.

## Reframing `bio-core-k12` and `oe-interdisciplinary-k12` as synergistic, not opposed

This is the single most important framing move in this document, so it's worth stating plainly rather than leaving it implied: **`bio-core-k12`'s caution about premature complexity and `oe-interdisciplinary-k12`'s evidence for early scaffolding opportunities are answers to different starting assumptions about classroom context, not contradictory claims about the same classroom.** `bio-core-k12` is a reasonable design for a minimal-prior-exposure classroom with no room for agency-adjacent content before the mechanism is secure. `oe-interdisciplinary-k12` is a reasonable design for a rich-scaffolding classroom with the time and support to work through agency-inclusive framing early and repeatedly. Neither LPM is wrong; each is under-specified about *which classroom it's built for*, because that assumption was never made explicit as data — it lived implicitly in each LPM's design philosophy instead. Making it explicit, via the trajectory mechanism above, is what would let both LPMs' real evidence base count as *converging* support for a context-aware ecosystem, rather than being read (as a first glance at "one has zero human content, the other has some") as one LPM simply being behind the other.

## A quantitative look

Full graph-theoretic and information-theoretic tooling (concept centrality, bridge detection, grade-band emphasis entropy, Jensen-Shannon divergence between LPMs) is Phase 4 scope and isn't built yet. The numbers below are hand-computed from the same source strand files cited throughout this document, as a preview of what that tooling will compute automatically and make live-browsable — offered now, with the calculation shown, so the claim is checkable today rather than only after Phase 4 lands.

**Grade-band distribution of human-relevant concept emphasis** (K-2 / 3-5 / 6-8 / 9-12), counting a substrand as "human-relevant" if it references Agency (`OE-CONCEPT-000211`) or names a human example directly:

| LPM | K-2 | 3-5 | 6-8 | 9-12 | Shape |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| `bio-core-k12` | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | No distribution — absence, not concentration |
| `oe-interdisciplinary-k12`, Agency concept (any framing) | 1 | 1 | 1 | 1 | Uniform — Shannon entropy = log₂(4) = **2.0 bits**, the maximum possible for four bands: "early and repeated" |
| `oe-interdisciplinary-k12`, human named explicitly | 0 | 0 | 0 | 2 (`000224`, `000234`) | Fully concentrated — entropy = **0 bits**: "held until 9-12" |

Read together, these three rows are the quantitative version of the "grounding" section above: `bio-core-k12` isn't a low-entropy version of the same distribution `oe-interdisciplinary-k12` has — it's a different kind of thing (no distribution at all). And `oe-interdisciplinary-k12` isn't simply "early" or "late" — it's early at maximum entropy for the general concept and simultaneously at zero entropy (fully late) for the human-named instance of that concept. A single number ("average grade band of human content") would have erased exactly the distinction that matters here. When Phase 4's Jensen-Shannon divergence measure exists, the natural next comparison is between `oe-interdisciplinary-k12`'s "Agency, any framing" distribution and its "human named" distribution — i.e., measuring how differently shaped a concept's general scaffolding is from its human-specific instantiation *within the same LPM*, not just across LPMs.

## Open questions

This document doesn't resolve any of the following — they're offered as the starting agenda for RFC discussion, not conclusions:

- Should the trajectory mechanism (Phase 3) live on `oe:SubStrand` as a new field, or is the existing `required` + `recommendedSequence` machinery sufficient with clearer documented conventions? (Phase 3 will draft a concrete proposal; this is where domain-editor and community input matters most before anything is locked in.)
- Is `bio-core-k12`'s human-content absence better resolved by adding a non-agentic human-lineage substrand (Phase 7 proposes a draft), or is a documented, deliberate absence itself a legitimate design choice worth leaving as-is, once it's *recorded* as deliberate rather than silent?
- Does `OE-CONCEPT-000211`'s formal definition need to be revised toward the more deflationary "without requiring conscious intention" framing before any new human-anchored content extends it, or should the strand-level restatement be brought back in line with the current formal definition instead? Both are live options; this document takes no position on which.
- Should `OE-STRAND-000224` be promoted from elective to required, given `OE-STRAND-000234` already assumes required-track students have encountered agency-spectrum reasoning it never itself develops? Or is the two-speed design (systems-level claim for everyone, individual-agency comparison for those who opt in) intentional and worth keeping?
- What would it take to build the integrated evolution-education / AI-literacy literature base that currently doesn't exist, and is that a research contribution OpenEvo/CCS is positioned to make directly, given the gap identified at the top of this document?

Discussion belongs in a `/proposals/` RFC thread once concrete schema or content changes are ready to propose (Phases 3 and 7); until then, this document itself is the record of the open questions, kept resolvable and visible per the same principle that keeps `contested` alignment records visible rather than suppressed.
